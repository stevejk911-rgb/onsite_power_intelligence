Accountability

We don't ask you to trust us. We publish whether we were right.

A power-feasibility call is only worth the track record behind it. So every screening verdict here is timestamped, and scored against what actually happens in the field. This page is the public scoreboard — and the realized base rates that anchor it.

Why this exists. Anyone can publish a confident number. The only thing that earns trust is being measurably, repeatedly right — and owning it publicly when we're not. Grid and on-site power projects take years to energize, so this ledger fills in over time. That's the point: it's falsifiable.

How a verdict gets scored

When a site is screened we record the coordinate, the inputs (MW, timeline), the verdict (ADVANCE / CAUTION / KILL), the fastest path and its month estimate, and the timestamp. A verdict is later marked:

Hit — the realized outcome fell inside our stated band (e.g., we said on-site ~22 mo and it energized within the screening tolerance; or we said KILL and the grid did arrive far outside the window).

Miss — reality fell outside the band. We publish the gap and the reason, and feed it back into the model.

Outcomes are confirmed from primary records — ISO/utility energization filings, operator disclosures, and verified developer reports — each with a source, the same way every number in the tool is sourced.

How the realized dataset grows (and becomes a moat)

Market- and utility-specific realized timelines don't exist cleanly anywhere — they live in tribal knowledge and scattered filings. We're assembling them two ways:

1. Completed-project mining. Realized request-to-energized durations computed from ISO/RTO queue records and LBNL data, market by market.

2. Verified developer contributions. Operators submit a real deal's timeline (applied date, energized date, utility) with evidence; we verify the source before it counts. Contributors are credited. Unverified claims never enter the dataset.
